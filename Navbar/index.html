This stuff may sound cheesy and obvious when I spell it out like this, but 99% of people will not take this advice and won't go out and try to improve upon their own skillset while also looking for a job. Will you? I want you to write this down, post it up on your mirror, and repeat it out loud every day: Be the one who goes out and does it. TODAY'S #ELB TASK LIST This is the definitely most vague and most important lesson, because only you can really think outside the box about what kind of skills you could speed up by yourself. 1. Go through the job descriptions you looked up during Lesson 1. Troll through the skill requirements and start breaking it down. How? Make a list in three columns: * the skills you definitely have * the skills kinda know but you could improve * the skills you don't know at all yet, but could potentially learn Focus on Column 2 first, so that you can move those skills into the "def have them" list. Then prioritise those skills you don't have yet and make a backlog, so that you can start slowly knocking them out over time. 2. Go through the LinkedIn employee profiles you looked up during Lesson 1. Look at the skills they highlight on their own profiles, and repeat the task above. 3. There's a worksheet in it for you to fill out, too. The first thing on our agenda is creating a target list of 10-15 employers. Just like when preparing for university applications back in high school, you need to know exactly where you’re applying before you put together an application. You wouldn't just apply to any Marketing course you saw, would you? You'd want to know where you were going to be studying. (Makes sense when I put it that way, doesn’t it?) This list is going to become your new holy scripture. It’s the place where you’re going to keep going back to, again, and again. Why? Because it’s the very best way to keep you from getting distracted. Think about it: you know that sending out resumes to every info@companyyoudontevenlike.com email address you can find is a waste of time. You know that most people get hired through that secret door. You do know this. And yet, like a bad job search junk food diet, we keep sending out those resumes anyways, don’t we? Because it makes us feel productive. And I don’t want you to simply feel productive. I want you to actually go accomplish things. WHERE TO START IF YOU DON'T KNOW WHERE TO START If you cannot name one single company you want to work for today, that’s totally okay. You’re not alone–and it’s understandable. A lot of times, when we start tailspinning into the job search stress, having any kind of job starts looking less and less appealing. Every company on your target list should meet two major requirements. It should operate in an industry you want to work in and a city you plan to live in. INDUSTRY VS. DEPARTMENT See, there are two axes to consider when you're deciding where to head next in your career. But we tend to only think about one of them, and in very broad ways. "I want a job in marketing." "I want to do events." "I want to be a graphic designer." These are all departments and job titles within any given company. Vague, vague titles. Which is why focusing on which industry you want to work in is where the real secret lies. It can sound counterintuitive and scary to limit yourself to a specific industry, I know. But we're dealing with a little psychology here. You want to position yourself as a professional and an expert in your field. And most experts are very, very niche about what they do. Their specialization is what makes them so impressive and revered. To give an example, I want you to think about hiring a wedding photographer. You have two choices: 1. "I'm a photographer. Yeah, I shoot all kinds of events. It's not super expensive to hire me, either. Actually, if you hire me for two events at once, I'll give you $100 off right now." 2. "I focus on outdoor, mountain-landscape wedding photography for bohemian brides. Last year, I shot weddings in Sedona and Park City and Switzerland. I grew up in the mountains myself, and I don't think there is a more beautiful location in the world for a wedding. I really like to capture the entire weekend so that you don't have to worry about missing a single moment." Clearly, Option #2 is the dream choice. Not only do you know exactly what they love doing, you know how it is going to benefit you: the photographer's employer. They share your same vision.
